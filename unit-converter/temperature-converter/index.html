What is a Temperature Converter?

Temperature is a physical quantity that expresses the degree of hotness or coldness. It's a measure of the average kinetic energy of the particles within a substance. Since different countries and fields of study utilize various scales, an accurate and easy-to-use converter is essential.

An online Temperature Converter is a practical tool that allows you to instantly translate temperature readings from one common scale to another. Exploring Common Temperature Scales

Our converter supports the most widely used and historically significant temperature scales:

Celsius (¬∞C)

The most common temperature scale globally. Water freezes at 0¬∞C and boils at 100¬∞C (at standard atmospheric pressure).

Fahrenheit (¬∞F)

Primarily used in the United States. Water freezes at 32¬∞F and boils at 212¬∞F.

Kelvin (K)

The SI base unit of temperature, an absolute thermodynamic scale where 0 Kelvin (absolute zero) is the point at which all thermal motion ceases. Used extensively in science and engineering.

Rankine (¬∞R)

An absolute temperature scale that uses Fahrenheit degree increments. 0¬∞R is absolute zero, similar to Kelvin, and is used in some engineering applications.

Reaumur (¬∞Re)

An older temperature scale where water freezes at 0¬∞Re and boils at 80¬∞Re. Though less common today, it's included for historical and niche applications.

Key Temperature Conversion Formulas

Understanding the relationships between scales is crucial:

  • Celsius to Fahrenheit: $F = C \times \frac{9}{5} + 32$
  • Fahrenheit to Celsius: $C = (F - 32) \times \frac{5}{9}$
  • Celsius to Kelvin: $K = C + 273.15$
  • Kelvin to Celsius: $C = K - 273.15$
  • Fahrenheit to Kelvin: $K = (F - 32) \times \frac{5}{9} + 273.15$
  • Kelvin to Fahrenheit: $F = (K - 273.15) \times \frac{9}{5} + 32$
  • Celsius to Reaumur: $Re = C \times \frac{4}{5}$
  • Reaumur to Celsius: $C = Re \times \frac{5}{4}$
  • Fahrenheit to Rankine: $R = F + 459.67$
  • Rankine to Fahrenheit: $F = R - 459.67$
  • Kelvin to Rankine: $R = K \times 1.8$
  • Rankine to Kelvin: $K = R / 1.8$

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

What is "absolute zero"?

Absolute zero is the lowest theoretical temperature, at which particles possess the minimum possible kinetic energy. It is 0 Kelvin (-273.15¬∞C or -459.67¬∞F). It's the starting point for absolute temperature scales like Kelvin and Rankine.

When should I use Kelvin?

Kelvin (K) is the preferred unit in scientific and engineering contexts, especially when dealing with thermodynamics, gas laws, and other phenomena where absolute temperature is critical. It avoids negative numbers and provides a direct relationship to energy.

Why does Fahrenheit have such "odd" freezing and boiling points for water?

The Fahrenheit scale was originally defined with 0¬∞F as the temperature of a specific salt-ice-water mixture and 96¬∞F (later adjusted to 100¬∞F) as the approximate normal human body temperature. The 32¬∞F and 212¬∞F points for water freezing/boiling were derived from this initial setup.
