What is a Power Converter?

In physics, power is the rate at which energy is transferred or converted. It's how quickly work is done or energy is used. Just like length or weight, power is measured in various units depending on the application and the system of measurement being used.

An online Power Converter is a practical tool that allows you to translate power measurements from one unit to another seamlessly. This is crucial when comparing specifications of engines, understanding the output of electrical devices, calculating heating or cooling capacities, or working across different international standards.

Key Power Units Explained

Our converter supports a comprehensive range of power units, covering most common applications:

Watt (W)

The SI derived unit of power, equal to one joule per second. Commonly used for electrical power (e.g., light bulbs, appliances) and general power measurements.

Kilowatt (kW)

1,000 Watts. Often used for larger electrical appliances, engines, and to measure electricity consumption (e.g., on utility bills).

Horsepower (hp)

A non-SI unit often used to measure the power of engines, motors, and turbines. We use mechanical (Imperial) horsepower, where 1 hp $\approx$ 745.7 Watts.

BTU/hour (BTU/hr)

Commonly used in the United States for measuring the cooling or heating capacity of HVAC (heating, ventilation, and air conditioning) systems.

Megawatt (MW)

1,000,000 Watts. Used for large-scale power generation, such as power plants or large industrial equipment.

Foot-pound/minute (ft-lb/min)

An imperial unit of power, representing the work done (foot-pounds) per minute. Used in some mechanical and engineering contexts.

Our converter also includes Gigawatt (GW) for extremely large power outputs, Milliwatt (mW) for small electronic devices, and energy-based units like Calorie/second (cal/s) and Kilocalorie/hour (kcal/hr) for thermal power.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

What's the difference between power and energy?

Power is the *rate* at which energy is used or transferred (e.g., Watts, hp). Energy is the *capacity* to do work (e.g., Joules, Kilowatt-hours, Calories). Think of it like this: Power is how fast you're running, while energy is how far you ran.

Are all "horsepower" units the same?

No, there are several definitions of horsepower! The most common are mechanical (or Imperial) horsepower ($\approx$ 745.7 W), metric horsepower (Pferdest√§rke, PS $\approx$ 735.5 W), and electrical horsepower ($\approx$ 746 W). Why is BTU/hr used for air conditioners?

BTU (British Thermal Unit) is a traditional unit of energy. When divided by time (per hour), it becomes a unit of power. BTU/hr is widely used in the US to rate the heating and cooling capacity of HVAC systems because it directly represents the amount of heat energy added or removed per hour.
