Explore Common Density Units

Density is typically expressed as mass per unit volume. Here are some of the most frequently used units, all supported by our versatile converter:

Kilogram/Cubic Meter (kg/m³)

The SI base unit for density. Widely used in scientific and engineering contexts globally.

Gram/Cubic Centimeter (g/cm³)

Often used for liquids and small samples, especially in chemistry. Note that water's density is approximately 1 g/cm³.

Pound/Cubic Foot (lb/ft³)

A common imperial unit, particularly in the US and UK, for materials and construction.

Pound/US Liquid Gallon (lb/US gal)

Useful for specifying the density of liquids like fuels and oils in the US.

Gram/Liter (g/L)

Often used in chemistry, especially for gas densities, as 1 g/L is equivalent to 1 kg/m³.

Kilogram/Liter (kg/L)

Convenient for measuring the density of liquids, as water is approximately 1 kg/L.

We also support specialized units like Pound/Cubic Inch (lb/in³), Ounce/Cubic Inch (oz/in³), Ounce/US Liquid Gallon (oz/US gal), and Slug/Cubic Foot (slug/ft³) to ensure all your conversion needs are met.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

What is density?

Density is a measure of mass per unit volume. It tells you how much "stuff" is packed into a certain amount of space. For example, lead is denser than wood because a piece of lead takes up less space for the same amount of mass.

Why is kg/m³ the standard (SI) unit for density?

Kilogram per cubic meter (kg/m³) is the SI derived unit because the kilogram (kg) is the SI base unit for mass and the cubic meter (m³) is the SI derived unit for volume. It's the internationally accepted standard.

How does specific gravity relate to density?

Specific gravity (SG) is a dimensionless quantity that is the ratio of a substance's density to the density of a reference substance (usually water at 4°C for liquids/solids, or air for gases). Numerically, the specific gravity of a substance is often very close to its density in g/cm³ or kg/L.
