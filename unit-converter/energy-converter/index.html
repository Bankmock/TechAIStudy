Explore Common Energy Units

Energy units vary widely depending on the context. Our converter supports a comprehensive range:

Joule (J)

The SI base unit of energy. Commonly used in scientific and engineering fields.

Calorie (cal / kcal)

There are two types: the small calorie (cal) used in physics, and the large calorie (kcal or Cal), which is what you see on food labels, equal to 1000 small calories.

Kilowatt-hour (kWh)

This is the unit your electricity bill uses! It measures the energy consumed by a 1,000-watt device operating for one hour.

British Thermal Unit (BTU)

Often used in the heating and air conditioning industry, representing the heat required to raise the temperature of one pound of water by one degree Fahrenheit.

Electronvolt (eV)

A tiny unit used predominantly in atomic, nuclear, and particle physics to quantify very small amounts of energy.

Foot-pound (ft-lb)

An imperial unit of work or energy, typically seen in mechanical engineering contexts.

We also support Kilojoule (kJ) and Erg for even broader utility.

Key Energy Conversion Formulas (Examples)

While our tool does the heavy lifting, here are some fundamental relationships:

  • 1 Joule (J) = 1 Newton-meter (N·m)
  • 1 Kilojoule (kJ) = 1000 Joules (J)
  • 1 Calorie (cal) $\approx$ 4.184 Joules (J) (also called gram calorie)
  • 1 Kilocalorie (kcal or Cal) = 1000 calories $\approx$ 4184 Joules (J)
  • 1 Watt-hour (Wh) = 3600 Joules (J)
  • 1 Kilowatt-hour (kWh) = 3,600,000 Joules (J) or 3.6 Megajoules (MJ)
  • 1 British Thermal Unit (BTU) $\approx$ 1055.056 Joules (J)
  • 1 Electronvolt (eV) $\approx$ 1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ Joules (J)

These precise conversion factors ensure the highest accuracy for every calculation.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

What is the difference between "cal" and "kcal" / "Cal"?

A "cal" (small calorie or gram calorie) is the energy needed to raise the temperature of 1 gram of water by 1°C. A "kcal" (kilocalorie) or simply "Cal" (food calorie, with a capital C) is 1,000 small calories. When you see "calories" on food labels, they are almost always kilocalories.

Why is kWh used for electricity bills instead of Joules?

The Joule (J) is a very small unit for typical household energy consumption. A Kilowatt-hour (kWh) is a much larger and more practical unit for billing purposes, making the numbers on your electricity bill more manageable (e.g., 500 kWh instead of 1.8 billion Joules).

Can I convert between energy and power?

Energy and power are related but distinct concepts. Power is the rate at which energy is transferred or used (e.g., Watts = Joules per second). While our converter handles units like Watt-hours (which are energy units derived from power and time), it's not a power converter itself.
